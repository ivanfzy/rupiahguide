Understanding local payment etiquette helps travelers interact respectfully and smoothly during transactions. While Indonesia is generally relaxed about foreign visitors, following certain practices shows cultural awareness and appreciation.

## General Payment Demeanor

**The Relaxed Approach:**
Indonesian transactions typically proceed at a relaxed pace. Rushing, showing impatience, or displaying frustration is considered poor form. Wait your turn, greet the vendor, and allow the transaction to proceed naturally.

**Basic Courtesies:**
- Greet with "Selamat pagi/siang/sore" (Good morning/afternoon/evening) when appropriate
- Smile and make brief eye contact
- Use "Terima kasih" (thank you) when receiving change or goods
- "Permisi" (excuse me) when interrupting or getting attention

**Patience with Process:**
Counting change may take time, especially with large bills. Allow the vendor to count at their pace. Hovering or repeatedly asking "How much?" creates pressure.

## Handling Money

**Presenting Payment:**
- Hand money directly to the person using your right hand
- Left hand supporting the right elbow is acceptable
- Avoid throwing or sliding money across counters
- Smooth out crumpled bills before presenting

**Receiving Change:**
- Accept with your right hand
- Count discreetly after stepping aside if needed
- Don't count loudly at the counter unless discrepancy suspected
- Pocket money before leaving the transaction area

**Bill Condition:**
Vendors may reject heavily worn, torn, or taped bills. If you receive damaged notes, you can usually spend them at larger establishments or exchange at banks. Don't attempt to pass clearly damaged bills to small vendors.

## At Different Types of Establishments

**Street Vendors and Markets:**
- Prices are often flexible—polite negotiation is normal
- Start with a friendly greeting and smile
- Don't negotiate if you don't intend to buy
- Small purchases don't require haggling (Rp5.000–20.000 items)
- Accept the offered price if it seems fair—excessive bargaining over small amounts is seen as petty

**Warungs (Local Eateries):**
- Pay after eating unless counter service
- Round up slightly as appreciation for good service (optional)
- If you're a regular customer, small gestures of loyalty are appreciated
- Don't linger excessively during busy periods if you've finished eating

**Restaurants:**
- Wait for the bill—don't shout "Check please" across the room
- Call staff with a subtle hand raise or "Permisi"
- Service charges are usually added to the bill; additional tipping is optional
- If paying by card, ensure the amount is correct before signing

**Shops and Retail:**
- Queue politely—cutting in line is unacceptable
- Wait to be served rather than grabbing items yourself in smaller shops
- Ask permission before photographing products
- Bags may need to be left at counter in some shops

## Tipping and Appreciation

**Tipping Culture:**
Tipping is appreciated but not mandatory in Indonesia. The practice is more common in tourist areas than in purely local establishments.

**Tipping Guidelines:**

| Situation | Amount (IDR) | Notes |
|-----------|--------------|-------|
| Restaurants (no service charge) | 5–10% of bill | Optional if service charge included |
| Hotel porters | Rp10.000–20.000 per bag | Hand directly |
| Housekeeping | Rp20.000–50.000 per day | Leave in room |
| Private drivers | Rp50.000–100.000 per day | For good service |
| Tour guides | Rp50.000–100.000 | For day tours |

**Tipping Not Expected:**
- Street food vendors
- Warungs
- Counter-service establishments
- Taxi/ride-hailing drivers (rounding up appreciated)
- Convenience stores

**How to Give Tips:**
- Hand directly to the person when possible
- Use both hands for larger amounts
- Accompany with "Terima kasih" or "Makasih banyak"
- Don't make a show of it—be discreet

## Bargaining and Negotiation

**Bargaining Guidelines:**

| Situation | Bargaining |
|-----------|------------|
| Traditional markets (pasar) | Expected |
| Street vendors (souvenirs) | Expected |
| Art and craft shops | Expected |
| Some tour services | Negotiable |
| Homestays/guesthouses | Often negotiable |
| Supermarkets and convenience stores | Fixed prices |
| Restaurants with printed menus | Fixed prices |
| Chain stores | Fixed prices |
| Transportation (meter/apps) | Fixed prices |
| Established tour operators | Published rates |

**Bargaining Etiquette:**
- Start with a greeting and some friendly chat
- Ask "Berapa harganya?" (How much is it?)
- Counter with 60–70% of asking price in tourist areas
- Move toward middle ground
- Maintain friendly demeanor throughout
- Accept gracefully if no agreement reached
- Don't bargain for items you don't want to buy

**Respectful Limits:**
- Don't negotiate over very small amounts (under Rp10.000)
- Recognize when a seller has reached their lowest price
- Remember that small differences matter more to local sellers than to tourists
- Walk away politely if the price doesn't work for you

## Sensitive Situations

**Receiving Incorrect Change:**
If you receive too little change:
- Point it out politely: "Maaf, kurang" (Sorry, it's short)
- Show the calculation if needed
- Most vendors will correct honest mistakes immediately

If you receive too much change:
- Returning excess change builds goodwill
- "Maaf, kelebihan" (Sorry, there's extra) while handing back

**When Cards are Declined:**
- Don't show frustration
- Try another card or switch to cash calmly
- "Saya coba kartu lain" (I'll try another card)

**Price Disagreements:**
- Accept that you can walk away
- Don't argue or become confrontational
- "Terima kasih, saya pikir dulu" (Thank you, I'll think about it)

## Religious and Cultural Considerations

**During Ramadan:**
- Be respectful if eating/drinking in public during fasting hours
- Some restaurants may have curtained areas for non-fasting customers
- Evening meal timing may be delayed until after sunset

**At Religious Sites:**
- Donations (Rp5.000–20.000) are customary at temples
- Place money respectfully in designated boxes
- Don't display large amounts when making donations

**Left Hand Taboo:**
The left hand is considered unclean in Indonesian culture. Always use your right hand for giving and receiving money, eating, and greeting.

## Group Payment Situations

**Splitting Bills:**
- Indonesian meals are often communal—sharing is normal
- One person often pays and others reimburse later
- Apps like Splitwise help track shared expenses
- If splitting at restaurant, inform staff in advance

**Paying for Others:**
- Offering to pay is a gesture of friendship
- "Saya traktir" (My treat) when offering
- Reciprocity is expected in ongoing relationships

**Being Treated:**
- Accept graciously with thanks
- Offer to pay next time
- Don't insist on paying if someone clearly wants to treat

## Technology and Modern Payments

**QRIS Payments:**
When paying via QR code:
- Wait for vendor to confirm amount
- Show successful payment screen before leaving
- Some older vendors may be unfamiliar with the technology—be patient

**Mobile Wallets:**
- GoPay, OVO, and DANA are increasingly accepted
- Top up in advance to avoid transaction delays
- Not universally accepted—always carry cash backup

**Card Payments:**
- Chip and PIN standard
- Contactless growing but not universal
- Signature required for some transactions
- Ensure card is returned promptly

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Don't:**
- Flash large amounts of cash in public
- Throw money or slam it on the counter
- Become visibly angry over small amounts
- Bargain aggressively at fixed-price establishments
- Use your left hand for transactions
- Ignore queue order
- Refuse to pay small rounding differences

**Do:**
- Carry small bills for convenience
- Be patient with counting and change-making
- Show appreciation for good service
- Learn basic Indonesian phrases for transactions
- Respect that haggling is not always appropriate

## Key Takeaways

1. **Use your right hand** for all money exchanges
2. **Greet vendors politely** before transactions
3. **Patience is valued**—don't rush the process
4. **Tipping is appreciated but optional** except in high-end tourist establishments
5. **Bargaining is expected** in markets but not in fixed-price shops
6. **Small gestures of respect** matter more than perfect execution
7. **Accept that mistakes happen**—handle discrepancies calmly
8. **Carry cash for small vendors** even if using cards elsewhere
9. **Learn basic phrases**—effort is appreciated even if pronunciation isn't perfect
10. **When in doubt, observe locals** and follow their lead
